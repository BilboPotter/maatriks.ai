There are hundreds of workout apps. Most of them are built for people who already know what they are doing. If you are new to the gym and just want something to tell you what to do, the list gets much shorter.

I tested seven apps that claim to work for beginners. This is not a ranking. Different apps suit different people. I am going to tell you what each one actually does and where it falls short so you can pick the one that fits.

What a beginner actually needs from a workout app

Before the list, here is what matters when you have never followed a program:

  • It tells you what to do. Not a library of 500 exercises to browse. A plan for today.
  • It does not assume you know things. If the app says "do 3x10 RDLs at RPE 8" on day one, it was not made for you.
  • It responds to what you actually do. If you skip a day, swap an exercise, or go lighter than planned, the app should notice and adjust. A PDF with exercises does not do that.

The apps

Fitbod

What it does well: Fitbod creates a new workout for you each session based on what muscle groups need work, what equipment you have access to, and how recovered you are. The interface is clean. Exercise videos are high quality and shot from multiple angles. It tracks your history and increases weight gradually over time.

Where it falls short: The free tier is limited - you get three workouts before you hit the paywall. At roughly 13 dollars a month, it is one of the more expensive options. The AI makes good choices most of the time, but it does not explain why it chose what it chose. You trust the plan or you do not.

Best for: Beginners who want variety in every session and have equipment access (gym or decent home setup).

Price: Around $13/month or $80/year.

StrongLifts 5x5

What it does well: Dead simple. Three exercises per workout, three workouts per week, always barbell compounds. Squat, bench, row, overhead press, deadlift. The app tells you exactly what weight to use and adds 2.5 kg every session. There is almost nothing to think about.

Where it falls short: The program is rigid. If you miss a day or stall on a lift, the only option is a built-in deload - not an actual adaptation. It is also barbell-only, which can be intimidating for someone who has never touched a barbell. No exercise variety at all.

Best for: Beginners who want the absolute simplest starting point and have access to a barbell rack.

Price: Free tier available. Premium around $10/month.

Ray

What it does well: Voice-guided coaching. You put your phone down and Ray talks you through the workout like a trainer standing next to you. It uses your camera to count reps automatically. The tone is encouraging without being over the top, and it explicitly targets people who feel intimidated by the gym.

Where it falls short: At 20 dollars a month, it is premium-priced. The voice feature means you need headphones in a busy gym. And the rep counting, while impressive, is not always accurate - especially for less standard movements. Workout programming is less detailed than dedicated strength apps.

Best for: Beginners who want the feeling of a trainer without the cost of a real one, and are comfortable with voice guidance.

Price: $20/month. 7-day free trial.

Hevy

What it does well: Primarily a workout logger, and a good one. Clean interface, fast to use mid-set, and has social features so you can follow friends and share workouts. The free tier is generous. Hevy recently added an AI workout generator, but the core product is the tracking.

Where it falls short: It does not tell you what to do out of the box. You need to find or build a program yourself, then log it in Hevy. The AI features are bolted on, not central to the experience. If you want a plan handed to you, Hevy alone is not it.

Best for: Beginners who already have a plan from somewhere (a friend, a blog post, a coach) and want a solid way to track it.

Price: Free tier. Premium around $3/month.

Nike Training Club

What it does well: Free. Fully free. The workout library covers strength, HIIT, yoga, and mobility, with video walkthroughs led by real trainers. Beginner-specific programs exist and are easy to find. If you are unsure whether you even like working out yet, this is a zero-risk way to try.

Where it falls short: No adaptation. The app gives you a schedule, and you follow it. It does not know what weight you used or whether the workout was too easy. It also leans more toward bodyweight and general fitness than structured strength training.

Best for: Complete beginners who want a free, no-commitment starting point. Good for figuring out what kind of training you enjoy before paying for a specialised app.

Price: Free.

FitnessAI

What it does well: Adapts weight, sets, and reps based on your logged history. Claims to draw from over 100 million data points. Sessions can be as short as 5 minutes or as long as you want. The app is opinionated - it tells you exactly what to lift.

Where it falls short: The interface feels cluttered. It throws a lot of data at you early on, which can be overwhelming if you do not know what any of it means yet. The tone is aggressive and data-heavy - less "here is your plan" and more "STOP GUESSING. START PROGRESSING." Not everyone responds to that.

Best for: Beginners who like numbers and want a data-forward approach from day one.

Price: Subscription via app stores. Roughly $10-15/month.

maatriks

What it does well: You answer a few setup questions - how often you can train, how long your sessions are, what you want to focus on - and the app builds your first week. After each workout you log what you actually did, including any exercises you swapped or sets you skipped. The app then writes a short summary explaining what it will change next time and why. The feedback loop is the point: every session informs the next one.

Where it falls short: Newer than the others on this list, so the exercise library and community are smaller. No voice guidance. No social features. If you want a large community or content beyond the workouts themselves, this is not it yet.

Best for: Beginners who want a plan that actually responds to what they do, with clear explanations for why the plan is changing.

Price: Details on maatriks.ai.

So which one should you pick

If you have never worked out before and want to spend nothing, start with Nike Training Club. It is free and the guided programs will teach you whether you like strength training, HIIT, or something else.

If you know you want to lift and want the simplest possible program, StrongLifts will tell you exactly what to do with a barbell three days a week.

If you want an app that builds the plan for you and adjusts it over time, Fitbod and maatriks both do that. Fitbod has been around longer and has a bigger exercise library. maatriks is newer but explains why your plan changed after each session, which can help you learn what is actually working as you go.

If you already have a plan and just need a place to log it, Hevy is the best tracker on the list and the free tier covers most of what you need.

There is no single right answer. The best app is the one you actually open when you get to the gym.
